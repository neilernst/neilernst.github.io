On Workshops, Conferences, and Archival Research Publication

Author

Neil Ernst

Published

June 6, 2021

One of the important aspects in science is communication with other scientists (and the public who fund it, but that’s a separate topic). Historically this happened when the elite sent letters to one another, or attended the wealthy gentleman’s club called the Royal Society. Once the research was mature, it was released as a book because you could make money that way. Darwin famously rushed out The Origin of the Species so as to compete with other outlets - there was no preprint server for him to establish precedence.

Nowadays in computing researchers typically have four main options for publication (we tend not to write letters to one another).

  • Preprint servers
  • Workshops
  • Conferences
  • Journals (peer reviewed)

Of these options the first is not peer reviewed formally, but if the paper is important, it will get reviewed on social media or via OpenReview and other forums. This is now the main avenue for dissemination of fast-moving results like in machine learning.

Typically the idea is that new ideas get submitted to workshops as short papers with preliminary results (I am including ERA/NIER (early research/new ideas) tracks at conferences in this definition). The more substantive version is now published as a conference paper with a validated, interesting, novel idea. Finally, extensions and enhancements might be reported in a longer journal article (with 40% or so new content). If the result is new enough, or the field fast-moving, one might skip that entirely and publish on Arxiv first, then look for an archival venue.

I want to talk a bit about how we should do research communication. I think there are several actual objectives:

  • Circulate new ideas and approaches for feedback: This is like beta testing software: you have some notion of how it ought to work and now need some experts to comment on what you (and your group) might have missed.
  • Archive results for others to reuse: the research result is completed and now we want to disseminate our brilliant findings, datasets and methods to others. We need some sort of long-lived resource to reference.

The problem I find is that we have working sessions where researchers in a small niche get together to talk about research approaches, problems, etc. These tend to be 10-40 people large, usually co-located with other conferences. The objective for these should be to circulate ideas!

But after a while those workshops, if they are successful, attract more and more attendees and submissions. The organizers now have the happy problem of fitting all the papers into a single day, so they might end up ‘rejecting’ papers which should really just be about circulating. Then the steering committee says they could become a working conference with a longer, maybe 2 day program. The steering committee - who are usually vested in the success of the sub area—starts to think about becoming independent and archival. Papers which come up with new ideas are shunted into separate tracks.

But now as an archival destination the conference is stuck. It isn’t big enough to play with the big kids with their A* ratings, so they don’t get submissions from academics motivated by rankings. It also isn’t helping to circulate the ideas, since we are rejecting half or more of the papers. To get the vaunted A* ranking, the conferences must have high-quality (read: high h-index) academics on the organizing committee, and reject a lot of papers (to show they are selective).
