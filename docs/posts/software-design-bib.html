Annotated Design Bibliography

design
annotated-bib
Author

Neil Ernst

Published

October 13, 2023

The purpose of this annotated bib is to capture what people have researched on software design, what the current research areas are, and where the field is headed. The bigger context is to understand, given AIDEs, how developers will a) use these to solve design challenges, e.g., implement asynchronous data retrieval; b) understand the solution that Copilot or whatever has selected; c) contextualize that to the problem at hand.

Early Studies

  • (Guindon 1990)
  • (Curtis, Krasner, and Iscoe 1988) - Visited 9 companies and did 97 interviews (!!) on the design process. Findings were that 1) hard to build domain knowledge, as opposed to tech knowledge: “They didn’t have enough people who understood warfare to assess what a war actually meant”. Exceptional designers understood the domain very well, excellent communicators, and drve the project to succeed. Managers would rapidly lose touch with the technical problems. 2) fluctuating requirements, eg. custom builds for important customers 3) communication breakdowns. “Brief reports of this research have ap- peared in the proceedings of the Ninth International Conference on Software Engineering, the Eleventh NASA Software Engineering Workshop, and the Second Workshop on Empirical Studies of Programmers.”

Design Detection

Automated Design Detection and Mining

  • (Galster, Treude, and Blincoe 2019) - a late breaking idea that proposes to gather information sources to issue recommendations on design. They propose knowledge graphs. This paper might help motivate practical implications. A bit before AIDEs so does not account for that support.
  • Soliman’s studies on SO
  • (Bi, Liang, et al. 2021) looked at QA tactics in SO. They used the literature to develop a dictionary of common QA/AT terms to search for. They then use info gain to add more terms. We should look into why this method might find new SO posts we don’t identify (and if that matters?). The approach is generic and not project specific (so it finds anything about heartbeat and not necessarily a specific issue.) Posts are at https://github.com/QA-AT/Mining-QA-AT-Knowledge-in-SO

Finding Design Patterns

  • (Wijerathna et al. 2021) - uses Design Context to capture the “external relevant info”. Adds a taxonomy for Design Pattern Context that seems fairly broad (e.g., `Platform Context’). A smple ML approach can classify DC posts pretty accurately. Seems to still miss the “so what” answer; just generates associations between design pattern and design context. Data: https://github.com/laksW/Mining-and-Relating-Design-Contexts-and-DesignPatterns-from-Stack-Overflow
  • (Mirakhorli_2016?) looked for Tactics in source code

Finding and Sharing Architecture Knowledge

Mechanically

  • (Gorton et al. 2017) - Use QUABASE to capture automatic NoSQL knowledge. Mentions “non-features”, things a product does NOT implement, as hard to identify. They use ML to find relevant URLs for human annotators. The classifier finding a relevant URL (either top result, P@1, or lower) was ok, but not great, and varied greatly, as the docs varied.

Curating knowledge for QuABaseBD requires deep expertise in distributed systems and databases. It is also labor- intensive and, as we show in this paper, error-prone.

  • Latoza, Programming Strategies work

Qualitatively

  • (Bi, Ding, et al. 2021) - how projects - Hibernate and ArgoUML - share knowledge. Looks at mailing lists and manually extract posts related to architecture. Table 5 is the summary of the qualitative analysis (what is communicated).They comment on who, when, and what was communicated. As (might be) expected, most communication happens early in the project. Lots of info communicated via email but rarely committed to a formal Doc.

  • (Ko and Chilana 2011) - Looked at bug reports in Firefox, Linux, Facebook to see how design was communicated. They classify high volume bug reports and find some dimensions of discussion, including the notion of rhetorical device (e.g. hyperbole). Lots of the discussions involved rationale, why decisions should be made (appropriate in a bug report). Little use of evidence.

  • (Power and Wirfs-Brock 2019) - looks at how decisions are made. Surveyed 62 architects. They use the Naturalistic Decision Making model/theory: Team Coordination, Inadequate Info, Time Pressure, Poorly Defined Procedure, Dynamic Changing Conditions. THen classify responses into that model. That tells us how decisions might be made and what mitigations people take when e.g. they are not an expert (‘recognition-primed’)

  • (Petre, Hoek, and Bowers 2019) - Looks at different dialogues that occur in design: “We characterise this management of perspectives in terms of design dialogues. These dialogues intersect, contrast, and represent different, simultaneous perspectives on design – and accommodate shifts in the design space as the design evolves. The dialogues may be within the designer’s mind, between a designer and some external representation, or among colleagues.” Examples are problem-solution, concrete-abstract, and dialogue with team members.

Design Assistants

  • Reflexion Models as one-off reconstructions of the actual design, used to investigate the violations.
  • ArchJava where one uses ArchJava to express the design, and that is then enforced with rules.
  • CAST etc have ways to creating a model of the design, that can then be detected. An “Architecturally Evident Coding Style” could be used to enforce rules such as “modules in com.example.ui cannot talk to com.example.model”

My sense for the latter two is that these are both over constrained - they create rules people invariably need to break, since the domain is not well understood; and under constrained, in that the language is not expressive enough to restrict everything one might want to restrict.
